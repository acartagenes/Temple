The bridge between dreams and reality
Step into the virtual.
An exploration of the unknown, and the unimaginable.
The quality of being 3 Dimensional truly asks for the collaborative involvement of the viewer in answering the question: What if? What started as a playful curiosity, has blossomed into excavating the deepest layers of my mind. In creating this environment, I'm connecting with my first project and prior artwork; bringing out my imagination that survives on the development and the desire to see this project come to fruition. 3 weeks ago, I embarked in a collaborative learning experience with my best friend, Frank, where we started to play around using the program Blender. After many youtube videos later, plenty of coffee, and a patience I didn't think I had, I found myself getting closer to bringing to life something I saw faintly in my dreams.
I hope you enjoy the visuals, the colors, and most of all, Temple Art.

Center Piece (video to the left)

Ideas transition so much during the creative process. I created the head using a Face Builder Add-on, in which I used 6 different angled photos to mold my features into the head. After sculpting, picking the location, the material, and adding different features like "grass-hair", there is also an elimination process. (If you see the skunk, that means Blender crashed...)

Monstera Plant (video to the right)

After weeks of practice with youtube tutorials, I started creating more challenging models. Using many of my house plants as references, and messing around with the different blender commands, this helped me create various designs.

Building this from scratch was a slow process at first. I had never used Blender and after our professor introduced WebGL, I became very interested in recreating my own objects. So I dove deeper into the exploration. Youtube has incredible content creaters and if you are interested I would suggest following CG Fast Track, and CGMatter. Here are a couple of different scenes and images of the working process.

The last major portion of the project was to render an animated movie. At 24 frames per second, the new objective became matching movement to a 2 min song. First, we had to calculate and break down each section of the melody. Second, figuring out a composition that told a story, and captured each of the elements in the space. Third, playing around with location, rotation, and camera focal length brought up its own dilemas. Finally, once we were satisfied with the story, I sent it out to processing(Frank's computer). Roughly a 2 minute video turned into 3,048 Frames. Rendering speed was on average 70 seconds. So, 3,556 mins later (about 2.5 days of agonizing patience) the movie was complete!

We escape in books, in video games, in artwork and in many other forms. Building this virtual escape felt like I was in control of something, especially during a time with so many uncertainties. Temple Art became a way to visualize a space for my artwork. Yet, it ended up being an extention of the saturated colors, the mix of nature and body, and the possibility of queer spaces.
